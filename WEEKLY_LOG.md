### How the Complete System Works (End-to-End Pipeline)

Every trading day, the scheduler wakes up at 6:30 AM Eastern. First it checks if GitHub Actions pushed fresh model weights overnight — the CS system retrains CrossMamba and LightGBM every two weeks on Linux servers because CrossMamba's selective state-space model segfaults on macOS ARM. If new models are available, it pulls them and clears the model cache so the next prediction cycle uses the updated weights.

At 7:00 AM, the Intel Agent runs. It connects to Alpaca's market data API and fetches real-time snapshots for macro instruments — VIXY as a VIX proxy, TLT and SHV for the yield curve, HYG and LQD for credit conditions, UUP for the dollar, GLD for gold. It computes regime indicators: is VIX in crisis territory? Is credit tightening? What percentage of tracked stocks are advancing? Simultaneously, three news gathering agents run in parallel — one scanning macro headlines (Fed, economic data, geopolitical), one scanning sector-specific news (industry trends, regulatory changes), and one scanning company-level events (earnings, insider activity, M&A). All news feeds through Claude Haiku for context-aware sentiment scoring — not keyword matching, but actual understanding that "beat estimates but lowered guidance" is negative. The sentiment is scored, classified by event type, and weighted by temporal decay so a one-hour-old article matters more than a day-old one. SEC EDGAR is queried for recent Form 4 insider filings — insiders buying their own stock is one of the strongest known predictive signals. FRED is checked for any economic data releases. All of this is aggregated into a structured MarketBriefing object.

At 10:00 AM — not 9:30, because the first 30 minutes of trading are dominated by opening noise and the opening range hasn't formed yet — the daily cycle begins.

The ML model runs first. The signal bridge loads the CrossMamba model from disk (or falls back to LightGBM on Mac), initializes the CS system's SignalGenerator, and calls generate_signals(). This triggers the full feature engineering pipeline: 5 years of historical prices for 460 stocks, 200+ features including momentum at six horizons, mean reversion z-scores, realized volatility, volume surprises, 40 fundamental signals, 36 cross-asset signals, 10 sentiment signals, 10 feature interactions (value times momentum, quality times value, momentum times low volatility — each based on published academic research), and fractionally differentiated price features for stationarity. The IC stability filter screens these down to the 65 most consistently predictive features across three non-overlapping time periods. The model then ranks all 460 stocks by predicted risk-adjusted forward return over the next 10 trading days. The output is a dictionary mapping every ticker to a score — positive means expected to outperform, negative means expected to underperform.

These predictions feed into the agent debate. Five analyst personalities receive the same inputs — the ML predictions, the market briefing, and the current portfolio state — but weight them differently. The Momentum Analyst puts 35% weight on model score dispersion and 30% on market breadth, reflecting a trend-following philosophy. The Risk Analyst puts 30% on drawdown proximity and 30% on VIX elevation, reflecting capital preservation. Each analyst produces a conviction score between 0 and 1, recommended portfolio parameters (how many longs, how many shorts, what leverage), and structured reasoning points. All five run in parallel.

Three PM personalities then blend the five analyst theses. The Aggressive PM trusts momentum and sentiment analysts most, proposing more positions at higher leverage. The Conservative PM trusts risk and macro analysts most, proposing fewer positions at lower leverage. The Balanced PM weights all analysts equally. Each PM produces a proposed parameter set: number of long positions, number of short positions, gross leverage, volatility target, sector neutrality constraints.

The CIO makes the final call. It first checks the HMM regime detector — a three-state Gaussian Hidden Markov Model trained on macro observables that classifies the market as bull, sideways, or bear with probability distributions. If the HMM is confident (over 60%) that we're in a bear regime, the CIO selects the Conservative PM regardless of what the analysts said. If we're confidently in a bull regime, it selects Aggressive. Safety overrides are hard-coded: VIX above 35 or drawdown beyond 10% always forces Conservative. This decision requires human approval on the first run of each day.

Once approved, the Execution Agent takes the PM's parameters and the ML rankings and constructs the target portfolio. It selects the top N stocks for long positions and the bottom N for short positions (where N comes from the PM's decision), sizes them using risk parity (inverse volatility weighting so each position contributes roughly equal risk), normalizes to the approved leverage level, and applies sector neutrality constraints. It then diffs the target against current Alpaca positions and computes the trades needed.

Before any order is placed, execution safeguards run. The system checks for unusual volume (possible pump and dump), abnormally wide spreads (market maker pulled quotes), price anomalies (possible stop hunting), momentum ignition patterns, market-wide stress (SPY down more than 3%), wash trade risk, and fat finger protection. Pre-trade risk limits enforce maximum gross exposure at 200% of equity, maximum single position at 10%, maximum daily loss at 3% before halting, and maximum sector concentration at 30%.

Orders that pass all checks are submitted to Alpaca with idempotent client order IDs to prevent double-trading on retry. Each order is polled for fill confirmation — the system doesn't fire-and-forget. If the fill price deviates more than 2% from expected, an alert is raised. Large orders (over $10k notional) are VWAP-split into 5 child orders executed over 10 minutes to reduce market impact. Every filled order is recorded in the SQLite-backed P&L tracker with strategy attribution.

From 10:15 AM onward, the intraday scanner runs every 15 minutes. It fetches one-minute bars from Alpaca and checks all 28 liquid stocks for four signal types: VWAP reversion (price extended beyond volume-weighted average), opening range breakout (price breaks the first 30-minute range), momentum burst (sudden directional move with volume confirmation), and gap analysis (overnight gaps that either fill or continue). Each signal uses ATR-adaptive thresholds — TSLA gets wider bands than Procter & Gamble because their normal intraday ranges are completely different. Signals are filtered by ML model conviction: the system only takes long intraday trades on stocks the daily model likes, and only takes short trades on stocks the model dislikes. Conflicting signals are discarded. A correlation filter prevents taking five tech stocks simultaneously when they're all just tracking the same sector move.

Intraday position management is asymmetric based on the overnight premium effect — research shows most equity returns happen overnight, not intraday. So intraday longs have tighter trailing stops and faster profit-taking (lock 60% of profit at 60% of target), while intraday shorts get wider stops and slower profit-taking (lock only 40% at 75% of target). All intraday positions are mandatorily closed at 3:45 PM.

During power hour (3:00 to 3:30 PM), scan frequency increases to every 5 minutes because momentum accelerates into the close.

At 4:05 PM after the market closes, the P&L tracker records a daily snapshot — equity, cash, all positions, daily return, cumulative return, rolling 30-day Sharpe, maximum drawdown. Position reconciliation runs against Alpaca to verify the system's internal state matches the broker — if there are discrepancies (missing positions, phantom positions, quantity mismatches), alerts fire.

At 4:30 PM, the feedback loop runs. Every analyst thesis and PM decision from the past 10 days is scored against realized returns. Did the Momentum Analyst's bullish call actually correlate with positive returns? Did the Risk Analyst correctly predict the drawdown? Scores are computed and used to update adaptive weights via exponential decay — analysts who were right recently get more influence, those who were wrong get less. The decay factor is 0.95 (slow, stable adaptation) with an 8% minimum weight floor so no analyst is ever fully silenced. PM trust weights adapt similarly.

At 5:00 PM, the intraday model collects today's one-minute bar data, builds microstructure features (order flow imbalance, Kyle's lambda, VPIN toxicity proxy, volatility signature ratios), labels trades using the triple barrier method (did price hit the profit target, stop loss, or time limit first?), and retrains a LightGBM model on the accumulated data. A meta-labeling model also trains — it predicts whether the primary model's signal will be correct, and uses that probability to size bets proportionally.

On Fridays at 4:15 PM, Claude generates a weekly institutional research report — a full Goldman-style strategy note that references actual portfolio positions, market regime, analyst convictions, sector exposure, and forward outlook.

Every 14 days, GitHub Actions triggers automated model retraining. It clones the CS system on a Linux runner, fetches fresh price data from Alpaca, fundamentals from yfinance, and runs a single-pass training of CrossMamba and LightGBM on all available data with the full feature pipeline (interactions, risk-adjusted targets, insider features). The retrained models are verified for pickle compatibility, committed, and pushed. The scheduler picks them up the next morning at 6:30 AM.
